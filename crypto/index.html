Caesar Cipher

The Caesar cipher is a very simple type of cipher. Take each letter of the input and shift it down the alphabet by a certain number of letters. For example, A shifted by 3 becomes D. For the purposes of this, the alphabet "wraps around"; for example, Z shifted by 3 becomes C.

This, of course, is a very easy cipher to break, as there are only 26 possible ways to shift a piece of text, and those can all be tried easily. Try encrypting a piece of text below, then try decrypting it by simply increasing the shift value until the text makes sense.

Vigenere Cipher

The Vigenere Cipher improves upon the Caesar Cipher by adding a set of shifts, often represented by a cipher word. Each letter in the cipher word (e.g. LEMON) is converted to the number of its place in the alphabet minus one (e.g. A becomes 0, E becomes 4, L becomes 11). Then, the plain text is aligned with the cipher word like so:

ATTACKATDAWN LEMONLEMONLE

Notice that the cipher word is repeated until it reaches the length of the plaintext. Each corresponding letter in the plain text is converted by shifting it by the value of the letter in the cipher word. For example, A shift by L (11) becomes L, T shifted by E (5) becomes X, and so on.

Vigenere Cipher, Shift, Vigenere Cipher

This method encrypts the text by first applying the Vigenere cipher, then transposing the result by moving each letter a number of places to the right (e.g. |ATTACKATDAWN| becomes AWN|ATTACKATD), then applying the Vigenere cipher again.

This is significantly worse than doing Vigenere cipher twice with different keywords, and only very slightly better than a single Vigenere Cipher. The second cipher can be broken as easily as a single Vigenere Cipher, and then the key can be deduced.
